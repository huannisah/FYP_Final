# Evidence-Based Coping Strategies for Mental Wellbeing

## Breathing and Grounding

**4-7-8 breathing**: Breathe in for 4 counts, hold for 7, breathe out for 8. Repeat 3–4 times. Helps reduce anxiety and improve sleep.

**5-4-3-2-1 grounding**: Name 5 things you can see, 4 you can touch, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste. Use when you feel overwhelmed or dissociated.

**Box breathing**: Inhale 4 counts, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4. Repeat. Simple and effective for calming the nervous system.

## Mindfulness and Relaxation

**Body scan**: Lie or sit comfortably. Slowly focus on each body part from toes to head, noticing tension and consciously relaxing. Takes 10–15 minutes.

**Mindful observation**: Pick an object (plant, cup, view) and focus on it for 2–3 minutes. Notice colour, texture, shape. Brings you into the present.

**Progressive muscle relaxation**: Tense muscle groups for 5 seconds then release. Start with feet, move up the body. Reduces physical tension linked to anxiety.

## Behavioural and Daily Habits

**Routine**: Keeping a simple daily routine (wake, meals, sleep) supports mood and reduces decision fatigue.

**Physical activity**: Regular movement (walking, stretching, light exercise) improves mood and sleep. Even 10–15 minutes helps.

**Sleep hygiene**: Consistent sleep and wake times, no screens before bed, and a dark, cool room support better rest.

**Limit alcohol and caffeine**: Both can worsen anxiety and sleep. Reducing or avoiding them often helps.

## Cognitive Strategies

**Thought challenging**: Notice negative or catastrophic thoughts. Ask: “Is there another way to see this?” or “What would I tell a friend?”

**Worry time**: Set a short “worry window” each day (e.g. 15 minutes). When worries arise at other times, note them and return to them in that window.

**Gratitude or good-things list**: Each day write 3 small good things or things you’re grateful for. Supports a more balanced outlook.

## When to Use These

These strategies are for general stress, low mood, and mild to moderate anxiety. They are not a substitute for professional care. If symptoms are severe, persistent, or you have thoughts of harming yourself, please seek help from a doctor or mental health professional.
